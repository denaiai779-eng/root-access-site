Michigan, My Michigan
Poetry • Free Verse • 4 Stanzas

In spring, the rivers remember how to move.

Ice cracks and loosens its grip on the Kalamazoo,

and water rushes beneath the birch trees,

cold and dark as a held breath finally released.

Trillium push through last year's leaves

on the forest floor — white stars

scattered across the mud and moss.

The whole state exhales.

Summer belongs to the lakes.

Superior, Michigan, Huron, Erie —

they stretch so far you'd swear you see the ocean,

but this water is sweet, not salt.

Children sprint across the hot sand at Sleeping Bear,

their laughter carried off by wind.

Sailboats lean into the gusts near Mackinac,

and the dunes hold the warmth long after sundown,

like the land is saving it for later.

When fall arrives, it arrives all at once —

maple trees blazing copper, amber, and wine

along every road from Traverse City to Saginaw.

The orchards at the edge of town

hang heavy with Honeycrisp and McIntosh,

and the air smells like cider and cold mornings.

You can hear the crunch beneath your boots,

a thousand fallen leaves announcing every step.

Then winter takes the Upper Peninsula

and tucks it under a quilt of white.

Snow buries the pines up past their knees.

Lake Superior freezes at its edges,

ice shelves reaching out like open hands.

The Yoopers light their woodstoves

and tell stories by the window

while the wind sings across the Keweenaw,

and the whole north holds still —

waiting, quiet, certain

that spring will find its way back again.
