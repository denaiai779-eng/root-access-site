Math: Place Value Day 3 — Comparing Numbers

45 min
Today's Goal: Compare multi-digit numbers using place value. Use the symbols >, <, and = to show which number is greater, lesser, or equal.

How to Compare Multi-Digit Numbers

When you compare two numbers, you want to figure out which one is greater (bigger) and which one is lesser (smaller) — or if they are equal.

Step 1: Line the numbers up by place value. Make sure the ones are under the ones, the tens are under the tens, and so on.

Step 2: Start at the leftmost digit (the biggest place value). Compare those digits first.

Step 3: If the digits are the same, move one place to the right and compare again. Keep going until you find a place where the digits are different.

Step 4: The number with the larger digit in that place is the greater number.

Example 1

Compare 47,382 and 47,519

Ten thousands: 4 = 4 (same — move right)

Thousands: 7 = 7 (same — move right)

Hundreds: 3 < 5 (3 is less than 5 — STOP!)

Answer: 47,382 < 47,519

Example 2: Using a Number Line

On a number line, numbers get larger as you move to the right.

←   47,000 —— 47,200 —— 47,382 —— 47,400 —— 47,519 —— 47,600 —— 48,000   →

Since 47,382 appears to the left of 47,519 on the number line, it is the smaller number.

Symbols to remember:

  • > means "is greater than"   (example: 500 > 200)
  • < means "is less than"   (example: 200 < 500)
  • = means "is equal to"   (example: 350 = 350)

Tip: The open mouth of the symbol always faces the bigger number, like a hungry alligator eating the larger meal!

Grammar: Capitalization Rules

Capital letters are important! They help readers know when a sentence starts, and they show that a word is a proper noun — the specific name of a person, place, or thing.

Rule 1: Always capitalize the first word of a sentence.

Example

✅ The Great Lakes hold one-fifth of Earth's freshwater.

❌ the Great Lakes hold one-fifth of Earth's freshwater.

Rule 2: Capitalize proper nouns — the names of specific people, places, and things.

People

✅ Governor Whitmer lives in Lansing. (specific person)

❌ The governor lives in lansing. (Lansing is a specific city — capitalize it!)

Places

✅ We visited Sleeping Bear Dunes and swam in Lake Michigan.

❌ We visited sleeping bear dunes and swam in lake michigan.

Specific Things

✅ The Mackinac Bridge connects the two peninsulas.

❌ The mackinac bridge connects the two peninsulas.

Rule 3: Capitalize titles of books, movies, songs, and other works. Capitalize the first word and all important words (not short words like "the," "of," "and," "in" unless they come first).

Example

✅ We read a book called The Land Between the Lakes.

Science: Collisions and Energy Transfer

40 min
Today's Goal: Understand that when objects collide, energy transfers from one object to another. Observe how energy moves through a chain reaction and what happens when the chain is broken.
When Things Collide Science Reading

Imagine rolling a bowling ball down a lane. It rumbles forward, heavy and fast, loaded with kinetic energy. Then — CRASH! It slams into the pins. Some pins fly backward. Others spin sideways. A few might even jump into the air. The bowling ball did not carry those pins — it transferred its energy to them. That is called energy transferWhen energy moves from one object to another, usually through a collision or contact: when energy moves from one object to another through contactWhen two objects touch each other; energy often transfers through contact.

The same thing happens on a pool table. When the cue ball strikes another ball, it slows down or even stops. Where did its energy go? It moved into the second ball, sending it rolling across the table. The cue ball's momentumA measure of how hard it is to stop a moving object; depends on both its mass (weight) and its speed. A heavy, fast object has a lot of momentum — a combination of its mass and speed — passed into the ball it hit. If that second ball hits a third ball, the energy transfers again. Energy does not disappear. It moves.

Now think about dominoes. You stand a line of dominoes on end, one behind the other. You push the first one, and it falls into the second. The second falls into the third. One by one, each domino passes its energy to the next, creating a chain reactionA series of events where each one causes the next one to happen — a series of events where each one causes the next. The first domino had very little energy, just a small push from your finger. But that tiny push was enough to tip domino after domino, all the way to the end.

What happens if you remove a domino from the middle of the line? The chain stops. The falling domino before the gap has no neighbor to absorbTo take in or soak up energy from another object its energy, so the reaction ends. This shows something important: energy transfer requires contact. Without contact, energy cannot move from one object to the next. Today, you are going to test this idea yourself.
