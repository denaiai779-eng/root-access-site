This is from Week 1, Thursday — Social Studies. Michigan Geography.

Social Studies — Hook
Say this:

"Look at your hand — palm up. Now look at this map of Michigan. See anything similar? That shape is called the Lower Peninsula — and people have been calling it 'the mitten' for as long as anyone can remember."

Say this:

"But before anyone called it Michigan, the Anishinaabe people — the Ojibwe, Odawa, and Potawatomi — had their own names for this land. They called the big lake michi-gami — the great water. That's where the name Michigan comes from."

Students then draw and label their own Michigan map with both English and Indigenous place names — Detroit from Anishinaabe "Waawiyaatanong," Mackinac from "Michilimackinac."

Math — Bug Check

When your student struggles, the curriculum tells you exactly what kind of struggle it is:

Error 404 — They don't get it at all. Go back to physical blocks. Build the number. Count it. Try a completely different angle.

Error 500 — They're trying but the reasoning is off. Find exactly where the logic breaks. Ask them to talk through their thinking.

Syntax Error — They understand the concept but make mechanical mistakes. More practice, not more teaching.

Questions you probably have.

Do I need a teaching background?

No. Every lesson includes scripted dialogue — word-for-word suggestions for how to introduce concepts, transition between activities, and handle confusion. You read it at breakfast, teach it by 8:30.

What does "de-colonized" actually mean here?

It means Indigenous peoples are presented as civilizations with governance, trade networks, and agency — not as footnotes before "real" history starts. It means the Great Migration, labor movements, and civil rights are taught through the eyes of the people who lived them. It means we don't do "Columbus discovered America."

What subjects are covered?

Math (Singapore Math methodology), Science (Next Generation Science Standards, hands-on), Social Studies (Michigan-specific, de-colonized), and ELA (reading, writing, speaking, and listening integrated across all subjects — no standalone "English class").

How many days a week?

Three — Wednesday, Thursday, Friday. About 3 hours per day. Monday and Tuesday are free for co-ops, field trips, music, or just being a kid.

Is this aligned to standards?

Yes. Common Core Math, Next Generation Science Standards, Michigan Social Studies GLCEs, and Common Core ELA. Every lesson tags exactly which standards it addresses. A full coverage map is included.

What if my kid is ahead or behind grade level?

Every lesson includes differentiation: what to do if the student is ahead (extension activities, harder problems) and what to do if they're behind (scaffolding, concrete re-entry points). The Bug Report system helps you diagnose exactly where they're stuck.

Will more weeks be available?

Yes. New weeks release monthly. The full curriculum covers 36 weeks (a complete school year). Subscribe to updates when you download Week 1 and you'll be first to know when new content drops.
